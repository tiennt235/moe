# Cardiac Valves Primer

## Overview
The heart has four valves that keep blood moving in one direction through its four chambers.
The two atrioventricular (AV) valves sit between the atria and ventricles; the two semilunar
valves guard the exits into the great arteries. Healthy valves open fully and close
completely, preventing backflow (regurgitation).

## The Mitral Valve
The mitral valve is the atrioventricular valve on the **left side** of the heart, between the
left atrium and the left ventricle. It has two leaflets (bicuspid). It opens to let
oxygenated blood fill the left ventricle and closes during systole so blood is pumped into
the aorta rather than leaking back into the atrium.

## The Aortic Valve
The aortic valve is the semilunar valve between the left ventricle and the aorta. It has three
cusps. It opens during ventricular systole to eject blood into the aorta and closes during
diastole. **Aortic stenosis** is a narrowing of this valve that obstructs outflow.

## The Tricuspid Valve
The tricuspid valve is the atrioventricular valve on the **right side** of the heart, between
the right atrium and the right ventricle. It has three leaflets and prevents backflow into the
right atrium when the right ventricle contracts.

## The Pulmonary Valve
The pulmonary valve is the semilunar valve between the right ventricle and the pulmonary
artery. It opens to send deoxygenated blood to the lungs and closes to prevent backflow into
the right ventricle.

## Valvular Disease
Two broad failure modes affect any valve: **stenosis** (the valve does not open fully,
obstructing forward flow) and **regurgitation** (the valve does not close fully, allowing
backflow). Mitral regurgitation and aortic stenosis are among the most common valvular
lesions in adults.
